**Capacitance** — the ability of a capacitor to store energy in an electric field; measured in farads (F).
**Capacitive reactance** — the opposition a capacitor offers to AC current flow, given by $X_C = 1 / (2\pi f C)$; measured in ohms but dissipates no power.
**Capacitor** — a component consisting of two conductors separated by an insulating dielectric; stores energy in an electric field between the plates.
**CEPT** — European organisation that harmonises telecommunications regulation; its agreement allows Class A holders to operate in member countries with minimal paperwork.
**CW (Continuous Wave)** — transmission mode where the signal is switched on/off to encode Morse code.
**Dielectric** — the insulating material between the plates of a capacitor; its properties affect capacitance value and maximum safe voltage.
**Dielectric constant** — a dimensionless material property describing how well a dielectric concentrates electric field; higher value → greater capacitance for the same geometry.
**DXCC (DX Century Club)** — premier amateur radio award for confirmed contacts with 100+ countries.
**DXing** — the activity of contacting as many different countries or locations as possible, often for awards.
**DXpedition** — an expedition to a rare or remote location for the purpose of operating a radio station there.
**eQSL** — electronic QSL card system at eQSL.cc, popular for its picture support.
**Electrolytic capacitor** — a polarised capacitor using a metal foil and conductive fluid; capable of very high capacitance values but suited only to DC applications.
**EME (Earth-Moon-Earth)** — using the moon as a passive reflector for amateur radio contacts; also called moonbounce.
**Farad (F)** — the SI unit of capacitance; practical components are rated in picofarads (pF), nanofarads (nF), or microfarads (µF/MFD).
**HAREC (Harmonised Amateur Radio Examination Certificate)** — internationally recognised certificate issued to Class A RAE candidates.
**Hamnet** — SARL special interest group that coordinates amateur emergency communications in South Africa.
**IARU (International Amateur Radio Union)** — worldwide federation of 160+ national amateur radio organisations, founded 1925.
**ICASA (Independent Communications Authority of South Africa)** — the South African spectrum regulator; sole authority for issuing amateur licences and callsigns.
**ITU (International Telecommunication Union)** — UN specialised agency that allocates global radio spectrum and develops telecommunications standards.
**LoTW (Logbook of The World)** — the primary electronic QSL confirmation system, used for award credits.
**MFD** — abbreviation for microfarad (µF) found stamped on capacitors; used because the µ symbol caused printing difficulties.
**Mode** — the method by which information is encoded onto a radio signal (e.g. CW, SSB, FM, digital).
**Net** — a regularly scheduled group gathering of amateurs on a specific frequency and time.
**QSL card** — a postcard confirming the details of a radio contact (QSO), required for most awards.
**QSO** — an amateur radio contact between two stations.
**RaDAR (Rapid-Deployment Amateur Radio)** — portable, rapid-setup amateur radio operation from field locations; pioneered in South Africa.
**RAE (Radio Amateur Examination)** — the South African examination required to obtain an amateur radio licence, administered by the SARL on behalf of ICASA.
**Rag-chew** — a long, informal amateur radio conversation.
**SARL (South African Radio League)** — the national amateur radio organisation in South Africa; a founding member of the IARU.
**Sked (schedule)** — a pre-arranged meeting between two amateur stations at an agreed time and frequency.
**SSB (Single Sideband)** — a phone mode that transmits only one sideband of an AM signal, used widely on HF for its efficiency.
**Third-party traffic** — messages originating from someone other than the licensed operator; prohibited on amateur radio except during emergencies.
**Transceiver** — a combined radio transmitter and receiver in one unit.
**Variable capacitor** — a capacitor whose capacitance can be adjusted by changing the overlap between rotor and stator plates; used as a tuning control in radio receivers.
**ZS callsign** — South African amateur radio Class A callsign prefix, followed by a region digit and unique letters.
